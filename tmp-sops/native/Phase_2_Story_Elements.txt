Phase 2: Story Elements — Making Copy Feel Real



Phase 1 gave you the structure - the blocks that hold a long-form ad together. But Structure alone doesn't convert.

You can nail every block and still write copy that feels hollow. Like a copywriter wrote it, not a person who lived it.

The difference between copy that gets scrolled past and copy that makes someone stop, read every word, and click — it's not the structure. It's the story elements inside the structure.

Character. Setting. Conflict. Low point. Transformation. Dialogue.

These make the reader forget they're reading an ad. They stop evaluating and start feeling.

This module shows you how.



Section 1: Character

Every story needs a narrator. Someone the reader is listening to.

The character you choose determines everything — how the reader relates, why they trust, what makes them keep reading.

Wrong character = they disconnect. Right character = they lean in.



Why character matters:

The reader isn't evaluating your product. They're evaluating the person talking to them.

Is this person like me?

Have they been where I am?

Or do they know something I don't?

The character establishes the frame. Everything after — the problem, the discovery, the transformation is filtered through who's telling the story.



Six character types:

Core dynamic

What it does

When to use

Mirror / Transformed peer 

"I was you" – had problem, now solved

Narrator is the reader – same struggles, same frustrations, same failed attempts



When feeling understood matters most. When they've tried everything and feel alone.

From the doctor copy:

The narrator is an Authority (ER physician for 20 years) combined with Skeptic Convert ("I was skeptical as hell").

This works because:

The product sounds unusual — needs credibility

The mechanism is scientific — needs someone who can explain it

The reader is skeptical — needs someone who was skeptical too

Choosing your character:

Ask:

Does my product need credibility or relatability?

Is my reader more likely to trust someone like them or an expert?

What's the dominant objection — and which character overcomes it?

Different characters, different reasons to believe. Choose the one that gives your reader the strongest reason to trust what comes next.



Check your copy:

Who is telling this story? Would your reader trust this person? Does this character match what your product needs?



Section 2: Theme 

Every story is about two things: what happens, and what it means.

The plot is what happens. The theme is what it means.



Why theme matters:

Without theme, you have a story about a product that worked.

With theme, you have a story about something universal — identity, love, control, hope, connection.

The reader finishes and they're moved. Not just convinced — moved. They don't just want the product. They want what the product represents.



Theme connects to emotional state:

In the Research module, you identified where your reader is emotionally. That emotional state carries a deeper question underneath — that's your theme.

What they're feeling

Fear / Anxious

"What if something is wrong and I don't know it?"

Hope — is change still possible for me?

Anger / Frustrated

"The system failed me. No one listens."

Justice — will someone finally see the truth?

Helplessness

"I can't fix this for someone I love."

Agency — can I finally do something that matters?



The theme is the question your reader is living with. Your story answers it.

How to use theme:

You don't state it directly. You don't write "this story is about redemption."

You let it emerge through:

The low point (where the question feels most urgent)

The transformation (where the question gets answered)

Dialogue (where others reflect the change)



In the doctor copy:

The theme is: Unseen danger - the system won't save you, you have to save yourself.

Opening: "Your body is a ticking time bomb. And no doctor is going to warn you until it's too late."

Low point: "They didn't know their body was falling apart from the inside."

Villain: "We treat the crisis. We don't fix the cause."

CTA: "Don't wait until you're in my ER."

The word "danger" appears once. But the theme “ something is quietly destroying you and you need to act before it's too late” runs through every beat.



Check your copy:

What emotional state is your reader in? What question are they carrying underneath? Does your story answer that question — not with words, but through the transformation?





Section 3: Setting and the Inciting Incident

Every story needs a moment that pulls the reader in. A specific scene they can see. A moment where normal life breaks.

Setting makes the scene real. The inciting incident makes it matter.

They work together. You don't write them separately — you write one scene that does both.



Why this matters:

Vague openings get scrolled past. "I was struggling with sleep" — there's nothing to see. Nothing happens. No reason to keep reading.

Specific scenes with a breaking moment lock attention. The reader sees it happening. They feel something shift. They need to know what happens next.



Setting = where and what it feels like

Specific details that put the reader in the moment:



Detail type

What it does

Example

Time

When this is happening

"Last month." "Third night." "Getting ready for work."

Place

Where this is happening

"In her bathroom." "In my ER." "On the floor."

Specifics

Details too precise to be made up

"52 years old. Healthy weight. No heart history."

Sensory

What you'd see, hear, feel

"Found her on the floor." "Tension in my shoulders I didn't know I had.""The blood on my white sheets.""That wet licking sound. Over and over."



Inciting incident = the moment everything changes

Before this moment, the character is living with their problem. Coping. Maybe ignoring it.

After this moment, they can't ignore it anymore. Something has to change.

This creates forward motion. The reader needs to know what happens next.



Type of Inciting incident 

What happens

Example

External shock

Something happens that can't be ignored

"She collapsed in her bathroom. Her husband found her on the floor."

Internal breaking point

They hit a limit they didn't know they had

"I sat on the closet floor for twenty minutes. Couldn't move. Something broke."

Close call

Something almost happens that terrifies them

"I almost sent a confidential email to the wrong person. My hands were shaking."

Realization

They see something clearly for the first time

"I overheard my husband say, 'It's like living with a ghost.'"



From the doctor copy:

"Last month, a woman came in. 52 years old. Healthy weight. No heart history. She collapsed in her bathroom getting ready for work. Her husband found her on the floor. He thought she was dead."

Setting details:

"Last month" — recent, happening now

"52 years old. Healthy weight. No heart history." — specifics that prove truth

"Bathroom getting ready for work" — mundane moment, familiar to reader

Inciting incident:

"She collapsed" — the moment everything breaks

"Her husband found her on the floor. He thought she was dead." — the emotional hit that makes it impossible to ignore

One scene. Both layers. The reader sees it AND feels the shift.



Where to place it:

Early. Usually within the first few paragraphs.

The hook grabs attention. The inciting incident creates momentum. Together, they make it impossible to stop reading.

If it comes too late, the reader has already drifted.



Common mistakes:

Mistake

Problem

No scene at all

"I was struggling for years." — Could work when targeting solution and product aware audience, but won’t convert for problem aware audience 

Generic scene

"She was at home when it happened." — Where? Doing what? This is a placeholder.

Scene but no incident

Vivid details but nothing changes. No forward motion.

Over-written

"The pale morning light filtered through the gauze curtains..." — Feels crafted. Radar goes off.

Details that don't work

Adding specifics for the sake of it. Every detail should ground, trigger memory, prove truth, or serve the emotion.

Doesn't match reader

Too extreme or too mild. Reader can't see themselves in it.



Check your copy:

Is there a specific scene the reader can see? Does something happen in that scene that breaks normal life? Is it early enough to create momentum?





Section 4: Conflict and Stakes



Story without conflict is just a sequence of events. Conflict is what creates tension. Stakes are what make the tension matter.



Conflict = the gap

The gap between where they are and where they want to be. The gap between what they're doing and what's actually working. The gap between what they believe and what's true.

Without a gap, there's no tension. Without tension, there's no reason to keep reading.



Stakes = the cost

What happens if nothing changes? Not "things stay the same." Things get worse.

Weak stakes: "I'll keep being tired."

Strong stakes: "Your body is a ticking time bomb. And no doctor is going to warn you until it's too late."

Stakes answer: why does this matter NOW?



How to build conflict and stakes:

Start with the current problem. Then escalate.

Question

What it reveals

What happens if this continues for another year?

The trajectory

What relationships are at risk?

The personal cost

What are they missing out on?

The opportunity cost

What's the worst case?

The fear underneath

Then make it feel inevitable without action. Not "this might happen" — "this is where this leads."



From the doctor copy:

Conflict established:

"She hadn't slept properly in years. Four, maybe five hours a night. Always exhausted. Always pushing through. And her body finally said enough."The gap: she's pushing through, but her body is breaking down.

Stakes escalated:

"When you don't sleep properly, your body goes into a state of chronic stress... Your blood vessels get inflamed... Your heart has to work harder... Your brain gets inflamed..."Not just "you're tired." Your body is destroying itself.

Stakes made inevitable:

"And the worst part? This happens slowly. Over years. You don't notice it until something breaks. Until you collapse in your bathroom and your husband thinks you're dead."This is where it leads. Not maybe. This is the path you're on.



Common mistakes:

Mistake

Problem

No real conflict

"I wasn't sleeping well and wanted to feel better." — No tension. Why should reader care?

Stakes too vague

"It was affecting my quality of life." — What does that mean? Reader can't feel it.

Stakes too extreme

"I almost died three times." — If reader's situation isn't that severe, they disconnect.

No escalation

Problem stated but never built. Same level throughout. No pressure, no release.

Check your copy:

Is there a clear gap? Do the stakes escalate? Does the reader feel where this leads if nothing changes?





Section 5: Low point 

The low point is the most important scene in your story.

This is the moment of maximum pain. The bottom. Where hope is lowest. Where they've tried everything and nothing has worked.

It has one job: make the reader think "that's exactly where I am."



Why the low point matters:

When the reader sees themselves in the low point, they're not observing the story anymore. They're in it. The character's escape becomes their potential escape.

Get it wrong and the whole story breaks. Either the reader thinks "that's not as bad as me" (so the solution won't work for them) or "that's way worse than me" (so this isn't relevant).

The low point is the hinge. Everything after depends on it.



What makes a strong low point:

Element

What it does

Example

Matches their experience

Not dramatized, not minimized — the actual texture of where they are

"I'd accepted that I was just going to be tired for the rest of my career."

Includes resignation

Not just frustration (still fighting) — quiet acceptance of a diminished life

"That's the deal you make when you become an ER doctor."

Voices their exact thought

The thing they say to themselves when alone with the problem

"You sacrifice your sleep to save other people."



From the doctor copy:

"I'd accepted that I was just going to be tired for the rest of my career. That's the deal you make when you become an ER doctor. You sacrifice your sleep to save other people."

This isn't dramatic. He's not at rock bottom. He's just... resigned. He's made peace with a life of exhaustion. That's where most readers are. Not crisis. Quiet resignation. "This is just how it is."

Another low point — the woman on sleep meds:

"I had a woman who'd been on sleep medication for 11 years. Couldn't sleep without it."

Eleven years. That's not crisis. That's a life organized around the problem.



Surface pain vs real pain:

The low point must speak to the real pain, not the surface version.

Surface pain

Real pain

"I want to sleep better."

"I'd accepted this was just my life now."

"I'm tired all the time."

"I have nothing left for my kids by 6pm. I'm surviving, not living."

"My body hurts."

"My mother had chronic pain for years. Doctors had her on anti-inflammatories that were destroying her stomach."

Surface pain is what they'll say out loud. Real pain is what they think at 2am.



The low point earns the right to offer hope:

You can't skip it. You can't rush through it.

If you don't go deep enough into the pain, the transformation won't land. The reader will think: "Easy for you to say. You don't understand how bad it really is."

But when you go deep — when you describe it with enough specificity that the reader feels it — then you've earned the right to show them a way out.

The depth of the low point determines the height of the transformation.



Common mistakes:

Mistake

Problem

Too dramatic

"I was suicidal and couldn't get out of bed." — Most readers aren't here. They disconnect.

Too mild

"I was a bit tired sometimes." — Reader thinks: that's not what I'm dealing with.

Generic

"I had hit rock bottom." — What does that look like? What did it feel like?

External only

"I lost my job and my wife left me." — Low points work better when they're internal, not just circumstances.



Check your copy:

Does your low point match where your reader actually is? Would they read it and think "that's exactly how I feel"? Or is it too extreme, too mild, or too vague?







Section 6: Transformation

The transformation is what they came for. It's the proof that change is possible. It's the picture of the future they want.

But most copy gets it wrong. Either oversells it (triggers skepticism) or undersells it (fails to inspire)



What makes transformation believable:

Element

Why it matters

Example

Specific, not general

"I felt better" means nothing. Specific details make it real.

"I was waking up actually feeling rested. Not groggy. Not dragging myself out of bed. Actually rested."

Progressive, not instant

Overnight transformations feel fake. Unfolding over time feels real.

"First couple nights, nothing dramatic. Third night, I slept through. By week two, the tension in my shoulders was gone."

Unexpected discoveries

Benefits they didn't anticipate feel true — not marketing.

"This tension I'd been carrying in my shoulders — I didn't even realize how bad it was until it was gone."





What makes transformation inspiring:

Element

Why it matters

Example

Paints a picture they want

Not just results — a life they want. A feeling they want.

"I felt like myself again. The self I forgot existed somewhere around year five of residency."

External validation

Someone else notices. Proves it's real, not imagined.

"My husband looked at me and said, 'What is going on with you? You seem like a different person.'"

Identity-level change

Not just circumstances changed — they changed.

"Even with the same brutal schedule. Same overnight shifts. But my body was actually recovering."



From the doctor copy:

The transformation unfolds progressively:

"First couple nights, nothing dramatic."

"Third night, I slept through without waking up at 3am. That hadn't happened in years."

"By the end of the first week, I was waking up actually feeling rested. Not groggy. Not dragging myself out of bed. Actually rested."

"By week two, this tension I'd been carrying in my shoulders — I didn't even realize how bad it was until it was gone."

"By week three, my husband looked at me and said, 'What is going on with you? You seem like a different person.'"

"I felt like myself again. The self I forgot existed somewhere around year five of residency."

Notice what's happening:

Progressive timeline (believable)

Specific details at each stage (not vague "felt better")

Unexpected discovery (shoulder tension)

External validation (husband notices)

Identity-level change (felt like himself again)



Common mistakes:

Mistake

Problem

Too fast

"Within 24 hours I was a new person." — Triggers skepticism immediately.

Too vague

"It changed my life." — How? What's different? This is a bumper sticker, not a transformation.

Only external

"I lost 30 pounds and got promoted." — What about how they felt? External without internal feels hollow.

No progression

"It worked." — Where's the journey? The doubts? The slow realization?

No external validation

Transformation only exists in narrator's head. No one else confirms it.

Overpromising

Exaggerated claims destroy credibility. One lie undoes the entire story.



Check your copy:

Is your transformation specific and progressive? Does it include unexpected discoveries? Does someone else validate the change? Would a skeptical reader believe this timeline?







 Section 7: Dialogue and Secondary Characters



Dialogue makes scenes real. Secondary characters make transformations believable.

Used well, they make copy feel like life. Used poorly, they make it feel like a screenplay written by a marketer.



What dialogue does:

Function

What it does

Example

Creates scenes

Reader is in the moment, not reading about it

"My husband looked at me and said, 'What is going on with you?'"

Sounds human

Real people speak in fragments, with their own voice

"Mom, I don't know what this thing is but I'm actually sleeping."

Breaks rhythm

One line of dialogue after a block of narration pulls attention

"'How's your sleep been?'"

Shows, not tells

Reveals what narration can't

"There you are. God, I missed you." — shows a marriage healing in six words



How to use dialogue effectively:

Rule

Why

Example

Use exact words, not paraphrased

"She said she noticed I was different" is weak. Exact words are vivid.

"What is going on with you? You seem like a different person."

Keep it short

Dialogue in copy isn't conversation. It's the one line that matters.

One or two sentences max.

Make it sound like speech

Contractions. Fragments. The way people actually talk.

"I don't know what this thing is but I'm actually sleeping."

Place at emotional peaks

Dialogue lands hardest at turning points.

The low point. The transformation. The moment someone notices.





What secondary characters do:

Function

What it does

Example

Validate transformation

Someone else notices the change — proves it's real, not imagined

Husband: "You seem like a different person."

Represent different reader segments

Each character is a mirror for a different reader

Wife (menopause). Son (anxiety). Mother (joint pain).

Provide proof without feeling like testimonials

Results woven into story don't feel like marketing

"I haven't seen him in 18 months. His blood pressure normalized."

Address different objections

Each character's result answers a different doubt

Mother didn't know what she was trying — not placebo.



From the doctor copy:

Each secondary character does specific work:

Husband: "What is going on with you? You seem like a different person. You're not dragging yourself around the house anymore." — Validates the transformation. External proof.

Mother: "She calls me asking what I gave her because her pain is better than it's been in a decade." — Different use case (joint pain). Didn't know what it was — rules out placebo.

Son: "Mom, I don't know what this thing is but I'm actually sleeping and I feel way less stressed." — Younger demographic. Doesn't understand the science. Still works.

Patient (58, heart palpitations): "Been in my ER four times in two years. I haven't seen him in 18 months. His blood pressure normalized. His doctor took him off one of his medications." — Specific details. Medical proof. Long timeframe.

Patient (11 years on sleep meds): "She cried when she told me." — Emotional proof. Extreme case resolved.

Each one is short. Sounds like something someone would actually say. Validates a different aspect.





Common mistakes:

Mistake

Problem

Dialogue that sounds written

"This product has truly changed my life in ways I never expected." — No one talks like this.

Too much dialogue

Copy becomes a screenplay. Dialogue loses its punch when overused.

Secondary characters without specificity

"People have seen great results." — Who? What results? This is a claim, not a character.

Everyone sounds the same

Every testimonial is "it's amazing, I feel great." No differentiation, no credibility.

Dialogue states the selling point

"Wow, this really works by reducing inflammation!" — No one says this. Dialogue captures reaction, not mechanism.



Check your copy:

Does your dialogue sound like speech? Do your secondary characters feel like real people with specific results? Does each one validate something different?







Before You Move On

You should have:

☐ Character — chosen a character type that gives your reader a reason to trust

☐ Theme — identified the deeper question your reader is carrying (connected to their emotional state)

☐ Setting + Inciting Incident — a specific scene the reader can see, with a moment that breaks normal life

☐ Conflict and Stakes — a clear gap, with stakes that escalate and feel inevitable

☐ Low Point — matches where your reader actually is (resignation, not just frustration)

☐ Transformation — specific, progressive, with unexpected discoveries and external validation

☐ Dialogue and Secondary Characters — sounds like speech, each character validates something different

If any box is empty, go back. These elements are what make the reader forget they're reading an ad.







Diagnosing Weak Story Elements in Your Own Copy

After you write a draft, run it through these questions:

Character: Would my reader see themselves in this person? Do they share the same doubts, same failed attempts, same internal dialogue? Or is this a generic "person with a problem"?

Setting: Can the reader see where this is happening? Is there a specific moment they're dropped into? Or is it vague and floating?

Conflict and stakes: Does pressure build? Does the reader feel what's at risk? Or is the problem stated and then left flat?

Low point: Does this match where my reader actually is? Would they read this and think "that's exactly how I feel"? Or is it too extreme, too mild, or too generic?

Transformation: Is it specific and progressive? Are there unexpected details? Does someone else validate it? Or is it vague "results" language?

Emotional beats: Is there a sequence? Does it move the reader from where they are to where they need to be? Or is it flat, or all one note?

Dialogue and secondary characters: Does dialogue sound like speech? Do secondary characters feel like real people with specific results? Or do they sound like testimonials in quotation marks?

Specificity: Numbers, timeframes, names, sensory details, actions — are they there? Or is the copy vague and general?

Where you find weakness, that's where the copy is leaking credibility.







That's Phase 2. You now understand the story elements that make structure come alive — character, setting, conflict, stakes, low point, transformation, dialogue, and specificity.

These are what make the reader forget they're reading an ad. These are what make them see themselves in the story. These are what make them want the transformation for themselves.
